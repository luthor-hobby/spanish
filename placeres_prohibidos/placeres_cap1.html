Placeres Prohibidos

Laurell K. Hamilton · Capítulo 1: Uno

Lectura glosada para el estudio del español — cada oración con glosa, vocabulario y notas gramaticales

Contexto del capítulo

Anita Blake — animadora de muertos, cazavampiros — recibe en su despacho la visita de Willie McCoy, un vampiro de poca monta al que conocía de antes de su conversión. Willie quiere contratarla para investigar una serie de asesinatos de vampiros en el Distrito. Ella se niega. El capítulo establece el mundo de la novela: los vampiros son legales en EE.UU. desde el caso Addison contra Clark, y Anita es la mejor del oficio aunque no le entusiasme trabajar para los nomuertos.

§ Presentación de Willie McCoy
01
Willie McCoy ya era un capullo antes de morir, y la muerte no lo había cambiado.
Willie McCoy had been a jerk before he died. His being dead didn't change that.
vocabulario
ya erawas already (imperfecto of ser)
un capulloa jerk, an idiot (vulgar slang, lit. "bud/cocoon")
antes de morirbefore dying (before he died)
no lo había cambiadohadn't changed him (pluperfect)
gramática
  • ya era + antes de — Ya with imperfect = "already was." Contrasts a past state (era) against a reference point (antes de morir). The implication: death was expected to change him, but it hadn't.
  • la muerte no lo había cambiado — Pluperfect (pretérito pluscuamperfecto): haber in imperfecto + participio. Expresses an action completed before another past moment. Death came and went; his character preceded and outlasted it.
  • capullo — A richly vulgar Spanish insult. Literally a flower bud or silkworm cocoon, used colloquially (especially Spain) for a stupid/obnoxious person. Register is informal/crude.
  • Opening hook: The sentence is deliberately shocking — the dead can still be judged for who they were. Sets the tone of sardonic first-person narration immediately.
02
Lo tenía sentado delante, con una chaqueta deportiva de cuadros que cantaba como una almeja, y pantalones de poliéster verde fosforito.
He sat across from me, wearing a loud plaid sport jacket. The polyester pants were primary Crayola green.
vocabulario
lo tenía sentadohad him seated / he was sitting (causative tener)
delantein front, across from (her)
la chaqueta deportivasports jacket, blazer
de cuadroschecked, plaid (lit. "of squares")
cantaba como una almejawas screaming/garish (lit. "sang like a clam")
el poliésterpolyester
verde fosforitofluorescent green (lit. "little phosphorus green")
gramática
  • tener + object + participio — Tener + noun/pronoun + past participle expresses a state that results from having arranged/caused something. Lo tenía sentado = "she had him seated / he was [being kept] seated."
  • cantar como una almeja — Colorful colloquial idiom for something garish or loud (clothing, colors). Lit. "to sing like a clam" — ironic since clams don't sing. The unexpectedness is the humor.
  • de cuadros / de rayas / de lunares — Patterns in Spanish are expressed with de + noun: checked (cuadros), striped (rayas), polka-dotted (lunares).
  • fosforito — Diminutive of fósforo (phosphorus/match). Used colloquially as an adjective for fluorescent/neon colors. The diminutive here conveys informal register, not small size.
03
Su pelo negro, corto y peinado hacia atrás con gomina, enmarcaba una cara delgada y triangular.
His short, black hair was slicked back from a thin, triangular face.
vocabulario
peinado hacia atráscombed back, slicked back
la gominahair gel/grease (Spain; gel also used)
enmarcarto frame
delgada y triangularthin and triangular
gramática
  • Participial phrase as appositive: corto y peinado hacia atrás con gomina sits between commas, functioning like a relative clause: "his hair, [which was] short and slicked back." The past participle peinado agrees with pelo (masc. sg.).
  • enmarcar — A vivid verb choice: "to frame." Literally used for picture frames (marco); here used metaphorically for how hair frames a face. Watch for this kind of metaphorical extension in literary prose.
  • Character sketch technique: Three physical adjectives build the image economically. The order — hair → face — mirrors how a camera (or eye) might move from outer to inner detail.
04
Siempre me había recordado a los personajes secundarios de las películas de gángsters, esos tipos que venden información, hacen recados y son desechables.
He had always reminded me of a bit player in a gangster movie. The kind that sells information, runs errands, and is expendable.
vocabulario
recordar ato remind of (transitive)
el personaje secundariosupporting character (lit. "secondary character")
hacer recadosto run errands
desechableexpendable, disposable (from desechar = to discard)
gramática
  • recordar a alguien + a + noun — "To remind someone of something." Me recordaba a… = "He reminded me of…" Don't confuse with recordar algo = "to remember something" (no personal a).
  • esos tipos que… — Demonstrative + relative clause as an appositive: "those guys who…" Acts as a parenthetical definition of personajes secundarios. Tipo (guy, bloke) is informal.
  • desechable — From des- (undoing) + echar (to throw). "Throw-away-able" → disposable. The same root gives desechar (to discard), desecho (waste). A rich word-family to know.
05
Claro que, como Willie estaba muerto, lo de ser desechable ya no contaba. Pero seguía vendiendo información y haciendo recados. No, morir no lo había cambiado demasiado.
Of course now that Willie was a vampire, the expendable part didn't count anymore. But he was still selling information and running errands. No, death hadn't changed him much.
vocabulario
claro queof course, admittedly
lo de serthe thing about being / the matter of being
ya nono longer, not anymore
contarto count, to matter (here: to apply)
seguía + gerundkept doing, was still doing
demasiadotoo much, all that much
gramática
  • lo de + infinitive/noun — Lo de nominalizes a phrase: "the thing about being X / the X part." Very productive in Spanish. Lo de ayer (the thing from yesterday), lo de siempre (the same old thing).
  • seguir + gerund — "To keep doing / to still be doing." Seguía vendiendo = "he was still selling." One of the most useful aspect constructions in Spanish. Contrast with dejar de + infinitive (to stop doing).
  • morir no lo había cambiado — The infinitive morir is used as the grammatical subject: "dying hadn't changed him." Infinitives as subjects are common in Spanish. Note the neuter pronoun lo = him/it (direct object).
06
De todas formas, por si las moscas, evité mirarlo directamente a los ojos; es lo que se suele hacer cuando se trata con vampiros.
But just in case, I avoided looking directly into his eyes. It was standard policy for dealing with vampires.
vocabulario
de todas formasanyway, in any case
por si las moscasjust in case (lit. "in case of flies")
evitéI avoided (pretérito of evitar)
directamente a los ojosdirectly in the eyes
se suele hacerone usually does / it's usually done
tratar conto deal with
gramática
  • por si las moscas — Idiom: "just in case." Lit. "in case of flies" — origin unclear, but widely used across Spain and Latin America. Equivalent: por si acaso (more neutral).
  • se suele + infinitive — Impersonal construction with soler: "one usually does, it's customary to." Se suele comer a las dos = "People usually eat at two." Soler expresses habitual action.
  • cuando se trata con — Tratarse con = to deal/interact with. The se makes it impersonal/passive: "when one deals with." Note: tratarse de = "to be about" (different meaning).
  • evitar + infinitive — "To avoid doing." Spanish uses the infinitive after evitar when the subject is the same. Compare with evitar que + subjunctive when subjects differ.
07
Si antes era un saco de mierda estándar, ahora era un saco de mierda que había regresado de entre los muertos, y esa categoría me resultaba nueva.
He was a slime bucket, but now he was an undead slime bucket. It was a new category for me.
vocabulario
un saco de mierdaa piece of crap (lit. "a sack of shit" — very vulgar)
estándarstandard, run-of-the-mill
había regresado dehad returned from (pluperfect)
entre los muertosfrom among the dead
me resultaba nuevawas new to me, struck me as new
gramática
  • Si antes… ahora… — Contrastive si: not conditional but concessive/contrastive: "if before X, now Y." This use of si introduces a premise to contrast with, rather than a hypothesis.
  • resultar + adjective — Like parecer, resultar links subject to predicate adjective: "turned out to be, struck me as." Me resultaba nueva = "seemed new to me / struck me as unfamiliar." More nuanced than ser or estar.
  • Parallelism for comic effect: Repeating "saco de mierda" with only the qualifier changed (estándar → que había regresado de entre los muertos) is a deliberate rhetorical move — the absurd escalation underlines Anita's deadpan wit.
08
Estábamos sentados en mi despacho, con el aire acondicionado como sonido de fondo. Las paredes azul celeste que Bert, mi jefe, consideraba relajantes, le daban un aire frío a la habitación.
We sat in the quiet air-conditioned hush of my office. The powder blue walls, which Bert, my boss, thought would be soothing, made the room feel cold.
vocabulario
el despachooffice (Spain; oficina = more general)
el aire acondicionadoair conditioning
el sonido de fondobackground noise/sound
azul celestesky blue (lit. "celestial blue")
relajantesrelaxing (plural, agreeing with paredes)
un aire fríoa cold feel/air (atmosphere)
gramática
  • estábamos sentados — Estar + past participle = resultant state: "we were [in the state of being] seated." Compare with nos sentamos (we sat down — the action).
  • daban un aire frío a la habitación — Dar + noun + a: "to give [something] to [something]." Dar un aire = "to give a feel/vibe." Related: dar miedo (to frighten), dar pena (to sadden).
  • azul celeste — Compound color adjective where the second word modifies the shade. These compound colors are invariable in Spanish: azul celeste, verde oliva, rojo sangre — no gender/number agreement.
§ Diálogo — Primer intercambio
09
—¿Te molesta que fume? —Preguntó. —Sí —dije—. Mucho.
"Mind if I smoke?" he asked. "Yes," I said, "I do."
vocabulario
molestarto bother, to annoy (like gustar)
que fumethat I smoke (subjunctive)
dijeI said (pretérito of decir — irregular)
muchoa lot, very much
gramática
  • ¿Te molesta que + subjunctive? — Emotional/evaluative verbs like molestar, gustar, importar, encantar take a subjunctive clause when the subject of the clause differs from the experiencer. Te molesta que fume = "It bothers you that I smoke." The indirect object (te) is the experiencer.
  • Spanish dialogue punctuation — Note the em dash (—) at the start of speech, and how the reporting verb (dije) is embedded mid-speech between two dashes. This is standard Spanish dialogue formatting.
  • Mucho as standalone sentence — In Spanish, single-word answers (adjectives, adverbs) can stand alone as complete sentences. "Mucho." = "A lot." Highly emphatic and economical.
10
—Joder, ya veo que no me vas a poner las cosas fáciles.
"Damn, you aren't gonna make this easy, are you?"
vocabulario
joderdamn / bloody hell (vulgar; very common in Spain)
ya veo queI can see that / I can already tell that
poner las cosas fácilesto make things easy (lit. "to put things easy")
gramática
  • joder — The quintessential Spanish expletive. Functions as an interjection ("damn!"), a verb (joder a alguien = to screw someone over), or a noun (el joder). In Spain it's so common it has partially lost shock value. In Latin America it's much more offensive.
  • poner + las cosas + adjective — "To make things [adjective]." Poner fácil/difícil algo a alguien = "to make something easy/hard for someone." Compare: ponerse (reflexive) = "to become."
  • vas a + infinitive — Near future (ir a + infinitive): "you're going to." In spoken Spanish this is far more common than the simple future tense for near-future intentions.
11
Lo miré a la cara un instante. Seguía teniendo los ojos marrones. Me pilló, y bajé la vista a la mesa. Willie se rió con un sonido breve y jadeante. Tampoco le había cambiado la risa.
I looked directly at him for a moment. His eyes were still brown. He caught me looking, and I looked down at my desk. Willie laughed, a wheezing snicker of a sound. The laugh hadn't changed.
vocabulario
mirar a la carato look in the face
un instantea moment, an instant
seguía teniendostill had (continuative aspect)
me pillóhe caught me (pretérito of pillar)
bajé la vistaI lowered my gaze
jadeantepanting, breathless
tampoconeither, not… either
gramática
  • pillar — Colloquial Spain for "to catch." Me pilló = "he caught me [doing something I shouldn't]." Also: pillar un resfriado (to catch a cold), pillar una mentira (to catch a lie).
  • bajar la vista — Idiom: to avert one's gaze, to look down. La vista = gaze/sight. Related: echar un vistazo (to glance), a primera vista (at first sight).
  • tampoco le había cambiado la risa — Complex sentence with the "like-gustar" structure: cambiar + le (indirect object = to him) + la risa (subject). "His laugh hadn't changed him either" → "his laugh hadn't changed for him / on him." The tampoco echoes "también no" = "not either."
12
—Eh, te doy miedo. Mola. —No es miedo; es precaución.
"Geez, I love it. You're afraid of me." "Not afraid, just cautious."
vocabulario
dar miedoto frighten, to scare (lit. "to give fear")
molacool, awesome (Spain slang, from molar)
la precaucióncaution, precaution
gramática
  • dar miedo — Like gustar: "to give fear [to someone]." Te doy miedo = "I give you fear" = "I scare you." The subject is the scary thing; the experiencer takes an indirect object pronoun.
  • molar — Quintessentially Spanish slang (especially Spain): to be cool, to rock. ¡Mola! = "Cool!" / "Awesome!" From mola mogollón (that's really cool). Not used in Latin America.
  • Anita's retort: "No es miedo; es precaución" — the semicolon separates two parallel clauses. The contrast (miedo vs. precaución) reveals her character: she acknowledges the danger but refuses to be seen as weak.
13
—No te molestes en negarlo; puedo olerlo casi como si me rozara la cara, la mente… Me tienes miedo porque soy un vampiro.
"You don't have to admit it. I can smell the fear on you, almost like somethin' touching my face, my brain. You're afraid of me, 'cause I'm a vampire."
vocabulario
molestarse ento bother doing (reflexive)
negarloto deny it
olerloto smell it (oler = to smell; irregular: huelo)
rozarto brush, to graze, to barely touch
me tienes miedoyou fear me (lit. "you hold fear of me")
gramática
  • no molestarse en + infinitive — "Not to bother doing." Reflexive: no te molestes en negarlo = "don't bother denying it." Contrast: molestar a alguien (to bother/annoy someone) vs. molestarse en (to bother/go to the trouble of).
  • como si + imperfect subjunctive — "As if it were touching…" Como si me rozara — the subjunctive is required after como si (always imperfect or pluperfect subjunctive). Introduces a hypothetical comparison.
  • tener miedo vs. tener miedo a — Tenerle miedo a alguien = "to be afraid of someone." Here: me tienes miedo = "you have fear of me / you fear me." The indirect object me = of me.
14
Me encogí de hombros; ¿qué podía decirle? ¿Cómo mentirle a alguien que huele el miedo?
I shrugged; what could I say? How do you lie to someone who can smell your fear?
vocabulario
encogerse de hombrosto shrug (lit. "to shrink one's shoulders")
podía decirlecould she say to him
mentirle ato lie to (someone)
huelesmells (present of oler — irregular: o→hue)
gramática
  • encogerse de hombros — Common idiom. Note the verb: encoger = to shrink/contract; with -se and de hombros = a shrug. Spanish has many idioms with body parts: cruzarse de brazos (to cross one's arms/do nothing), levantar la cabeza (to hold one's head up).
  • oler — Stem-changing verb: o → hue in stressed syllables. Huelo, hueles, huele… olemos, oléis, huelen. The h is added before ue to prevent the diphthong from sounding like güe.
  • Rhetorical questions: Two consecutive rhetorical questions emphasize helplessness. Both are embedded in narration (not dialogue), showing Anita's internal logic. This is characteristic of first-person noir narration.
§ El negocio — Willie propone el caso
15
—Uf, me muero por un cigarro. —Le empezó a temblar un lado de la boca. —No sabía que los vampiros tuvieran tics.
"Geez, I wish I had a smoke." The skin began to jump at the corner of his mouth. "I didn't think vampires had nervous twitches."
vocabulario
me muero porI'm dying for (hyperbolic craving)
le empezó a temblarstarted to tremble (for him — involuntary)
un lado de la bocaone side of the mouth
el tictic, involuntary twitch
gramática
  • morirse por — Hyperbolic expression of intense desire: "to be dying for." Me muero por un café = "I'm dying for a coffee." The reflexive -se intensifies the drama.
  • le empezó a temblar un lado de la boca — The involuntary body-movement construction: indirect object pronoun (experiencer) + verb + infinitive + body part as subject. "One side of his mouth started twitching on him." The body part is the grammatical subject; Willie is the indirect object experiencer.
  • no sabía que + imperfect subjunctive — Past negated belief takes subjunctive in the subordinate clause: no sabía que tuvieran = "I didn't know they had." Contrast: sabía que tenían (I knew they had — indicative after positive).
16
Se llevó la mano casi hasta los labios y me sonrió enseñando los colmillos. —Hay cosas que no cambian.
His hand went up, almost touched it. He smiled, flashing fangs. "Some things don't change."
vocabulario
llevarse la mano ato raise one's hand to
hasta los labiosup to the lips
sonreírle a alguiento smile at someone
enseñarto show (also: to teach)
los colmillosfangs (also: canine teeth)
gramática
  • los colmillos — Key vampire vocabulary! Colmillo = canine tooth or fang. Related: los incisivos (incisors), las muelas (molars), los colmillos de elefante (elephant tusks).
  • enseñando — Gerund as manner/simultaneous action: "smiling, while showing his fangs." Two simultaneous actions: sonrió (main) + enseñando (manner/concurrent). Note: enseñar = to show AND to teach — context determines which.
  • hay cosas que no cambian — Haber impersonal (hay) + relative clause. Note: the relative pronoun que takes indicative (no cambian) because the antecedent is specific (these particular things). Compare with subjunctive for hypothetical/indefinite antecedents.
17
Tuve ganas de preguntarle: «¿Y qué cambia? ¿Qué se siente al estar muerto?». Conocía a más vampiros, pero Willie era el primero al que había tratado antes y después de la conversión. Se me hacía raro.
I wanted to ask him, what does change? How does it feel to be dead? I knew other vampires, but Willie was the first I had known before and after death. It was a peculiar feeling.
vocabulario
tener ganas deto feel like doing, to want to
¿qué se siente al…?what does it feel like to…?
la conversiónthe (vampire) conversion/turning
se me hacía raroit felt/seemed strange to me
gramática
  • tener ganas de + infinitive — "To feel like doing." One of the most useful expressions in Spanish. ¿Tienes ganas de salir? = "Do you feel like going out?"
  • ¿qué se siente al + infinitive? — "What does it feel like to [do something]?" Al + infinitive = "upon doing / when doing." ¿Qué se siente al estar muerto? = "What does it feel like to be dead?"
  • el primero al que había tratado — Relative clause with personal a: since the antecedent (el primero) is a person who functions as a direct object, Spanish requires the preposition a. Al que = a + el que.
  • se me hacía raro — Hacérsele raro a alguien: "to seem/feel strange to someone." The reflexive se + indirect object me express the subjective experience. Imperfect = ongoing feeling.
18
—¿Qué quieres? —Contratar tus servicios. Y pagarlos, claro. Lo miré evitando los ojos. La luz le centelleó en el alfiler de corbata; era de oro auténtico. Antes, Willie no tenía cosas así. No le iba nada mal para estar muerto.
"What do you want?" "Hey, I'm here to give you money. To become a client." I glanced up at him, avoiding his eyes. His tie tack caught the overhead lights. Real gold. Willie had never had anything like that before. He was doing all right for a dead man.
vocabulario
contratarto hire, to contract
centellearto glint, to sparkle, to flash
el alfiler de corbatatie pin (lit. "tie needle")
el oro auténticoreal gold, genuine gold
no le iba nada malhe wasn't doing badly at all
para estar muertofor a dead man (lit. "given that he was dead")
gramática
  • para + infinitive (concessive) — Here para has a concessive/qualifying meaning: "given that, considering that." Para estar muerto = "considering he's dead / for a dead man." Compare with the purpose meaning: para comer = "in order to eat."
  • irle bien/mal a alguien — "To be going well/badly for someone." Like gustar: the indirect object is the experiencer. No le iba nada mal = "It wasn't going badly at all for him."
  • cosas así — "Things like that." Así as a demonstrative-like adjective: "like this/that." More informal than cosas como esas.
19
—Me dedico a levantar muertos. Eres un vampiro, Willie, ¿para qué quieres un zombi? —No. —Sacudió la cabeza con dos movimientos rápidos hacia los lados—; nada de vudú. Quiero que investigues unos asesinatos.
"I raise the dead for a living, no pun intended. Why would a vampire need a zombie raised?" He shook his head, two quick jerks to either side. "No, no voodoo stuff. I wanna hire you to investigate some murderers."
vocabulario
dedicarse ato do for a living, to be in the business of
levantar muertosto raise the dead (zombies)
sacudir la cabezato shake one's head
nada denothing to do with, none of
el vudúvoodoo
unos asesinatossome murders
gramática
  • dedicarse a — "To dedicate oneself to / to work as." ¿A qué te dedicas? = "What do you do for a living?" One of the most useful everyday phrases.
  • quiero que + subjunctive — The classic subjunctive trigger: verbs of wanting/wishing with a different subject in the subordinate clause. Quiero que investigues = "I want you to investigate." If same subject: quiero investigar (infinitive).
  • nada de + noun — "Nothing of / nothing to do with." Strong dismissal or exclusion: nada de alcohol (no alcohol whatsoever), nada de eso (none of that).
20
—Al grano, Willie. —Suspiré y le eché una ojeada al reloj de la pared—. Tengo que largarme dentro de quince minutos. No me gusta hacer esperar a los clientes cuando están solos en el cementerio; suelen ponerse nerviosos.
"Can we cut to the chase here, Willie? I have to leave"—I glanced at the wall clock—"in fifteen minutes. I don't like to leave a client waiting alone in a cemetery. They tend to get jumpy."
vocabulario
al granoget to the point (lit. "to the grain")
suspiréI sighed (pretérito of suspirar)
echarle una ojeada ato glance at (lit. "to throw a little glance")
el reloj de la paredwall clock
largarseto get out, to leave (colloquial)
el cementeriocemetery
ponerse nerviososto get nervous (become + adjective)
gramática
  • al grano — Idiom: "to the point / get to it." From threshing grain (separating the relevant from the chaff). ¡Ve al grano! = "Get to the point!"
  • largarse — Colloquial reflexive: to scram, to get out, to split. More emphatic than irse: implies urgency or abruptness. ¡Lárgate! = "Get out of here!"
  • suelen ponerse nerviosos — Soler + infinitive: habitual action. "They tend to get nervous." Soler is defective (no future or conditional). Ponerse + adjective: to become [emotional state]. Key: ponerse for involuntary emotional changes.
  • hacer esperar a — "To keep [someone] waiting." Hacer + infinitive + personal a: causative construction. Hizo reír a todos (he made everyone laugh).
§ Contexto — El mundo legal de los vampiros
21
—¿Sabes lo de los asesinatos de vampiros en el Distrito? —Estoy al tanto. —Habían hecho una carnicería con cuatro vampiros en la nueva zona de marcha; aparecieron con el corazón arrancado y la cabeza cortada.
"You know about the vampires that are getting wasted over in the District?" He made it a question, so I answered. "I'm familiar with them." Four vampires had been slaughtered in the new vampire club district. Their hearts had been torn out, their heads cut off.
vocabulario
estar al tantoto be aware, to be up to date
la carniceríamassacre, bloodbath (lit. butcher's shop)
la zona de marchanightlife district (Spain; lit. "party/action zone")
arrancadotorn out, ripped out
la cabeza cortadasevered head
gramática
  • hacer una carnicería — Idiomatic: "to massacre, to make a bloodbath." Carnicería (butcher shop) extended metaphorically. Common violent figurative language in Spanish narration.
  • aparecieron con el corazón arrancado — Aparecer + con + noun phrase: "to appear/turn up with [something in a state]." The past participles (arrancado, cortada) describe the resultant state of the body parts. They agree in gender/number with their nouns.
  • la zona de marcha — Cultural term for an entertainment/nightlife district. La marcha (the scene/action/nightlife) is very Spanish slang. ¿Dónde está la marcha? = "Where's the action/nightlife?"
22
Sólo habían pasado dos años desde el caso de Addison contra Clark. Aquel juicio nos había cambiado la forma de ver en qué consistía la vida y en qué no consistía la muerte. En los Estados Unidos se había legalizado el vampirismo.
It had only been two years since Addison v. Clark. The court case gave us a revised version of what life was, and what death wasn't. Vampirism was legal in the good ol' U.S. of A.
vocabulario
sólo habían pasadoonly [X time] had passed
el caso de… contra…the case of… v. (versus)…
el juiciotrial, judgment
consistir ento consist of, to lie in
se había legalizadohad been legalized (passive se)
el vampirismovampirism
gramática
  • nos había cambiado la forma de ver — Complex dative structure: cambiar + nos (indirect object, us) + la forma de ver (direct object, the way of seeing). The trial changed "our way of seeing" — the indirect object marks whose perspective changed.
  • en qué consistía — Embedded indirect question: "what life consisted of." Consistir en = to consist of. The indirect question is the object of la forma de ver.
  • se había legalizado — Passive se (pasiva refleja): the se construction makes the agent implicit. "Vampirism was legalized" (by the government/courts, unspecified). Very common in Spanish for describing social/legal changes.
23
El nuestro era uno de los pocos países que reconocían los derechos de los nomuertos. En las fronteras las pasaban canutas tratando de impedir la inmigración de vampiros extranjeros en… Bueno, en bandadas.
We were one of the few countries to acknowledge them. The immigration people were having fits trying to keep foreign vampires from immigrating in, well, flocks.
vocabulario
el nuestroours (possessive pronoun, masc. to agree with país)
los nomuertosthe undead (compound: no + muertos)
pasarlas canutasto have a terrible time (idiomatic)
en bandadasin flocks (birds); here sarcastically applied to vampires
gramática
  • pasarlas canutas — Colorful idiom: "to have a rough time of it." Canuta = a type of tube/pipe; the expression's origin is disputed but the meaning is clear. Las = an implicit pronoun referring to "the situation."
  • en bandadas — En bandada = "in a flock" (used for birds). Applied to vampires here it's comic — the narrator's dry wit reduces a serious immigration issue to an animal migration image. This kind of register mismatch is characteristic of Anita's voice.
  • el nuestro — Strong/standalone possessive pronoun: "ours." Nuestro país (adjective) vs. el nuestro (pronoun, standing alone). The article is required with the standalone form.
24
—Ya, pero a los polis les pasa lo mismo que a ti, Anita. ¿Qué coño os importa que haya un vampiro más o menos? Ninguna ley nueva va a cambiar eso.
"Maybe, but the cops feel like you do, Anita. What's one more dead vampire? New laws don't change that."
vocabulario
los polisthe cops (colloquial; policías formal)
les pasa lo mismo que athey're the same as, the same applies to them
qué coñowhat the hell (very vulgar)
importarto matter, to care (like gustar)
que hayathat there is/are (subjunctive of haber)
más o menosmore or less; here: "one more or one fewer"
gramática
  • ¿qué os importa que + subjunctive? — Importar works like gustar: "to matter to." ¿Qué os importa? = "What does it matter to you [all]?" The subordinate clause que haya requires subjunctive because it follows an emotional/evaluative verb.
  • coño — Very vulgar expletive (anatomical term) used as an intensifier. Extremely common in colloquial Spain; much more offensive in Latin America. ¿Qué coño…? = "What the hell…?"
  • ya — Multifunctional word. Here: "yeah, I know, but…" — acknowledgment + mild concession. Other meanings: already, now, soon. Context is everything with ya.
§ La negativa de Anita
25
Contemplé al vampiro que tenía delante y me encogí de hombros. ¿De verdad me daba igual que hubiera un vampiro menos? Quizá.
I stared at the vampire in front of me and shrugged. Did I really believe, what was one more dead vampire? Maybe.
vocabulario
contemplarto look at, to contemplate, to gaze at
me daba igualI didn't care, it was all the same to me
que hubierathat there was (imperfect subjunctive of haber)
quizámaybe, perhaps (+ indicative or subjunctive)
gramática
  • dar igual — "Not to care, to be all the same." Me da igual = "I don't care / it's all the same to me." Works like gustar with indirect object. Da igual (impersonal) = "it doesn't matter."
  • que hubiera — Subjunctive in indirect questions after evaluative expressions: me daba igual que hubiera = "I didn't care whether there was." After emotional/evaluative verbs, the subjunctive is triggered in the subordinate clause.
  • quizá(s) — "Perhaps." Can take indicative (more certain) or subjunctive (less certain): quizás es verdad (it's probably true) vs. quizás sea verdad (it may possibly be true). The subjunctive signals more doubt.
26
Sacudí la cabeza. Aquello explicaba por qué había concertado la entrevista el cerdo avaricioso de Bert. Sabía de sobra qué pensaba de los vampiros, pero el contrato me obligaba, como mínimo, a recibir a cualquier cliente que le hubiera pagado una señal, y mi jefe era capaz de todo por dinero.
I shook my head. That explained why Bert, the greedy son of a gun, had set up this meeting. He knew how I felt about vampires, but my contract forced me to at least meet with any client that had given Bert a retainer. My boss would do anything for money.
vocabulario
concertar una entrevistato arrange a meeting/appointment
el cerdo avariciosothe greedy pig (vulgar insult)
saber de sobrato know perfectly well (lit. "to know with surplus")
el contrato me obligaba athe contract required me to
como mínimoat minimum, at least

ser capaz de todoto be capable of anything
gramática
  • el cerdo avaricioso de Bert — [article + insult + de + name]: a very Spanish construction for insults/epithets. "That greedy pig Bert." El idiota de Juan = "That idiot Juan." The de links the descriptor to the name.
  • saber de sobra — "To know perfectly well." De sobra = in excess, more than enough → "to know more than enough to be sure." Related: sobrar (to be left over, to be in excess).
  • obligar a + infinitive — "To oblige/require to do." The contract as subject: el contrato me obligaba a recibir = "the contract required me to receive." Obligation verb + indirect object + a + infinitive.
27
—La policía se está ocupando del caso —dije, levantándome—, y ya le presto tanta ayuda como puedo. En cierto modo, ya estoy trabajando en el caso; os podéis ahorrar el dinero.
"The police are looking into it. I am already giving them all the help I can. In a way I am already working on the case. Save your money."
vocabulario
ocuparse deto handle, to take care of
prestar ayudato lend/give assistance
en cierto modoin a way, in a certain sense
ahorrarto save (money)
os podéis ahorraryou (pl.) can save yourselves (Spain vosotros)
gramática
  • ocuparse de — "To deal with, to handle." Reflexive + preposition de. La policía se está ocupando del caso = "The police are dealing with the case." Note: del = de + el.
  • os podéis ahorrar — Vosotros form (os = 2nd person pl. reflexive). This is specifically Castilian Spanish; Latin American Spanish uses ustedes + se. Os here is reflexive: "you can save yourselves [the money]."
  • levantándome — Gerund embedded in dialogue tag: "she said, getting up." The gerund marks a simultaneous action to the main verb (dije). Spanish frequently uses gerunds in this narrative role.
28
Se quedó sentado mirándome, muy quieto. No era la inmovilidad exánime de los que llevan mucho tiempo muertos, pero casi daba el pego. El miedo me subió por el espinazo y me llegó a la garganta.
He sat staring up at me, very still. It was not that lifeless immobility of the long dead, but it was a shadow of it. Fear ran up my spine and into my throat.
vocabulario
muy quietovery still, motionless
exánimelifeless, without animation (literary)
llevar + time + muertoto have been dead for [time]
dar el pegoto pass, to fool, to look convincing
el espinazothe spine, backbone
la gargantathe throat
gramática
  • llevar + time + muerto — Llevar + duration + past participle: expresses how long someone has been in a state. Lleva mucho tiempo muerto = "he has been dead for a long time." Very useful construction: lleva dos años viviendo aquí (he's been living here for two years).
  • dar el pego — Spanish idiom: "to fool people, to pass as genuine." Lit. from card games. Casi daba el pego = "it almost passed for the real thing."
  • el miedo me subió por el espinazo — Vivid physical description of fear. Note how Spanish often gives agency to abstract nouns: "fear climbed me" rather than "I felt fear climbing." The indirect object (me) is the person experiencing the sensation.
29
Reprimí el impulso de sacar el crucifijo que llevaba debajo de la blusa y echar a Willie del despacho. No sé por qué, pero me parecía poco profesional expulsar a un cliente con un objeto sagrado.
I fought an urge to draw my crucifix out of my shirt and drive him from my office. Somehow throwing a client out using a holy item seemed less than professional.
vocabulario
reprimirto suppress, to repress
el impulsothe impulse, urge
el crucifijothe crucifix
debajo de la blusaunder the blouse
echar a alguiento drive/throw someone out
expulsarto expel, to drive out
sagradosacred, holy
gramática
  • parecerme poco + adjective — "To seem un-[adjective] to me." Me parecía poco profesional = "it seemed to me [to be] unprofessional." Poco negates the adjective gently (poco profesional ≈ unprofessional, but softer than no profesional).
  • reprimir el impulso de + infinitive — "To suppress the urge to do." The infinitive depends on the noun impulso, not on the verb reprimir. Spanish uses infinitives where English uses gerunds: "the urge to pull out" not "the urge of pulling out."
  • echar a alguien — "To throw/kick someone out." One of echar's many meanings: echar una carta (to mail a letter), echar de menos (to miss someone), echar a correr (to break into a run).
§ La despedida — Amenaza velada

—Tengo por norma no trabajar para vampiros. —¿Y no te saltarías esa norma por nada? —Otra vez aquella manía de hacer que las afirmaciones parecieran preguntas. —Por nada del mundo.
"Tell them, whoever they are, that I don't work for vampires." "A firm rule?" Again he made it sound like a question. "Concrete."
vocabulario
tener por normato make it a rule, to have as a rule
saltarse una normato break a rule (lit. "to jump over")
la maníahabit, obsession, quirk
las afirmacionesstatements, assertions
por nada del mundofor nothing in the world, not for anything
gramática
  • ¿no te saltarías…? — Conditional of saltarse in a negative question: "wouldn't you break…?" The conditional here implies a hypothetical scenario. Negative questions in Spanish expect confirmation of the negative: "you wouldn't, right?"
  • hacer que + subjunctive — "To make [something] seem/appear." Hacer que las afirmaciones parecieran preguntas = "to make statements seem like questions." Hacer que = causative with subjunctive (different subjects).
  • por nada del mundo — Strong emphatic negation: "for nothing in the world / absolutely not." The del mundo extension maximizes the scope of the refusal. Related: en absoluto, bajo ningún concepto.

—Siento que hayas dicho eso, Anita. No les gustan las negativas. —Pues a mí no me gustan las amenazas. Y estás abusando de mi hospitalidad. —No es ninguna amenaza, Anita. Es la verdad. —Se arregló la corbata, se ajustó el nuevo alfiler de oro, irguió los hombros y salió.
"I wish you hadn't said that, Anita. These people don't like anybody telling 'em no." "I think you've overstayed your welcome. I don't like to be threatened." "It ain't a threat, Anita. It's the truth." He straightened his tie, fondling the new gold tie tack, squared his thin shoulders and walked out.
vocabulario
siento que hayas dichoI'm sorry you said (subjunctive in regret)

gramática
  • siento que + subjunctive — Verbs of emotion take subjunctive in the subordinate clause: siento que hayas dicho eso = "I'm sorry that you said that." The perfect subjunctive (hayas dicho) anchors the regretted action before the present moment.
  • pues — Contrastive discourse marker. "Well, for my part…" Introduces a counter-argument. Pues a mí no me gustan = "Well, I don't like…" The a mí is emphatic — stressed contrast with what Willie just said.
  • Exit choreography: Three reflexive verbs in quick succession (se arregló, se ajustó, irguió) paint Willie's exit as a performance of dignity — the vampire composing himself before leaving. Anita's narration notices every detail.

Cuando se marchó, cerré la puerta y me apoyé en ella. Me temblaban las rodillas.

vocabulario

  • Revelation of fear: The whole chapter Anita has projected cool competence. This line — her knees were shaking — is the authentic reaction she kept hidden. It rewards readers who noticed the undercurrent of fear through the scene.

gramática
  • heridas que se curan — Curar vs. curarse: curar = to heal (transitive, someone heals something); curarse = to heal (intransitive reflexive, something heals itself). La herida se cura = "the wound heals."
  • propio de — "Characteristic of, typical of." A una velocidad propia de los humanos = "at a speed typical of humans." Propio has many uses: mi propio (my own), en persona propia (in person), propio de (typical of).
  • World-building through narration: These factual asides (silver, vampires, healing) establish the rules of Anita's world without lengthy exposition. The first-person voice makes it feel like insider knowledge being shared with the reader.
